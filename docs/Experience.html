Field Experience

Map

Below is an interactive map of all the field work that I have done over the last 4 years across the world. The majority of this work has been conducted with the LEAF lab but the points in Tahiti and Moorea are from my Wildlands studies experience this past summer.

More details

Over the summers of 2024 and 2025 I was able to be a part of a field crew based out of Sequoia national park analyzing ancient forests response to fire in areas of low, medium, medium-high, and high severity burn. We measured water potential and hydraulic capacity (water availability within the plants, and amount of air within the xylem representing dead tissue.) and regeneration via germination count at plots from the beginning to the end of the summer.

Sequoia Naitonal Park

In June of 2024 I was able to go with my graduate student mentor Mike Burnett to Tetiaroa to complete installation of the sapflow sensors we had been building for the year previous. Sapflow sensors are devices that when installed into a plant can track their hourly water uptake. On this trip we were insterested if coconut palms found in mass on the island due to an abandoned settle palm plantation used more of the islands limited water resources than the native Pisonia Grandis tree. A full report of my analysis can be seen in the projects section.

In the summer of 2025 prior to going to Sequoia national park I was able to return to French Polynesia to complete a 6 week field study program, completing various short experiements including pattern of sediment deposition and tropical fish counts at local beaches.

French Polynesia

Other Experiences

Other field experiences I have participated in have been a long term data collection project with the LEAF lab on the campus owned reserve Sedgwick. This has involved the collection of leaves at predawn and midday followed by immediate pressure bombing to assess drought tolerance and landscape scale response to dry and wet years across Coast Live Oaks and Blue Oaks.

Work on the Santa Clara river and Dangermond preserve was primarily the installation of Sapflow sensors to track the transpiration rates of various species, in Santa Clara specifically looking at the average water consumption of non-native Arundo Donax.
